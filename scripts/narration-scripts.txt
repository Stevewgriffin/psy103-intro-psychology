If you've ever had a job interview, taken a skills assessment, gotten a performance review, or worked under a manager who made your life either better or worse, you've been on the receiving end of I-O psychology. There are three main branches. Personnel psychology deals with hiring, training, and evaluating employees. How do you select the right person for a job? Through structured interviews, standardized assessments, and job analyses that define what the position actually requires. The research consistently shows that structured interviews, where every candidate gets the same questions, are far more valid than unstructured ones, where the interviewer just "goes with their gut." Organizational psychology focuses on the human side of the workplace: motivation, job satisfaction, leadership, organizational culture. And human factors psychology is about designing tools, systems, and environments that work with human capabilities rather than against them. Think about why some software is intuitive and some makes you want to throw your computer. Good human factors design accounts for how humans actually think, perceive, and make errors. The key insight here is that workplaces are psychological environments. How they're structured, managed, and designed has enormous effects on productivity, satisfaction, and wellbeing. That's not touchy-feely stuff. That's bottom-line business impact.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide3.mp3|Let's dig deeper into workplace motivation and leadership, because these topics connect directly to what you learned about motivation in Week 4. Douglas McGregor described two management philosophies. Theory X assumes workers are inherently lazy and need to be controlled, monitored, and motivated by external rewards and punishments. Theory Y assumes workers are inherently motivated and creative, and that management's job is to remove obstacles and provide opportunities for growth. Most modern management research supports Theory Y, but plenty of organizations still operate on Theory X assumptions. Think about your own work experiences. Did your boss trust you to do your job, or did they micromanage every detail? How did that affect your motivation? Leadership research has identified two major styles. Transactional leaders focus on clear structures, expectations, and contingent rewards. Do the work, get the paycheck. Transformational leaders inspire followers to transcend their self-interest, creating a shared vision and fostering personal growth. The research shows that transformational leadership tends to produce higher job satisfaction, more organizational commitment, and better performance. Job satisfaction isn't just about money. It's about feeling valued, having autonomy, and finding meaning in your work. And when those things are missing, you get burnout. Burnout isn't just being tired. It's emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and a reduced sense of personal accomplishment. It's a genuine occupational health crisis, and it connects directly to the stress material we're about to cover.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide4.mp3|Let's talk about stress. And I don't mean the casual use of the word, like "I'm so stressed about finals." I mean the systematic psychological and biological response to perceived demands that exceed your resources. Richard Lazarus and Susan Folkman developed the transactional model of stress, which is the most widely accepted framework. It says stress isn't about the event itself. It's about how you appraise it. Two people face the same deadline. One sees it as a challenge and gets energized. The other sees it as a threat and falls apart. Same event, different appraisals, different stress responses. There are different types of stressors. Acute stress is short-term and intense: a car accident, a public speaking event. Chronic stress is long-term and grinding: poverty, a toxic relationship, an unsatisfying job. Daily hassles are the small irritations that accumulate: traffic, lost keys, arguments. And major life events, both positive and negative, like marriage, divorce, or job loss, require significant adjustment. The Holmes-Rahe scale tried to quantify this by assigning stress points to life events. The higher your score, the more likely you are to experience health problems. But here's what the research really emphasizes: perceived control matters enormously. When you feel you have some control over a stressor, its impact is dramatically reduced. When you feel helpless, even minor stressors can become overwhelming. That sense of control, or lack of it, is a major factor in whether stress makes you sick.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide5.mp3|Now let's look at what happens inside your body when you're stressed, because this is where the biopsychology from Week 1 comes back in a big way. Walter Cannon identified the fight-or-flight response. When your brain perceives threat, the sympathetic nervous system kicks in. Adrenaline and noradrenaline flood your system. Your heart rate increases, your blood pressure rises, your muscles tense, your pupils dilate. Blood flow shifts from your digestive system to your muscles. You're ready to fight or run. This is adaptive. It kept our ancestors alive. The problem is that our bodies can't tell the difference between a charging predator and a work email from your boss at ten PM. The same system activates for both. The HPA axis is the slower, longer-lasting stress pathway. The hypothalamus signals the pituitary gland, which signals the adrenal cortex to release cortisol. Cortisol mobilizes energy, suppresses non-essential functions, and helps you deal with the immediate crisis. Hans Selye described this progression as the General Adaptation Syndrome: alarm, resistance, and exhaustion. In the alarm stage, your body mobilizes. In resistance, you adapt and cope. But if the stress continues long enough, you reach exhaustion, and your body starts breaking down. Chronic cortisol exposure suppresses your immune system, damages the hippocampus, which ironically impairs your memory and ability to regulate stress, increases cardiovascular risk, and promotes inflammation. This is why chronic stress literally makes you sick. It's not in your head. It's in your biology.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide6.mp3|So if stress is this powerful and potentially destructive, what can you actually do about it? Coping strategies fall into two broad categories. Problem-focused coping targets the source of the stress. If you're stressed about a deadline, you make a plan, break the task into smaller pieces, and start working. If you're stressed about a relationship, you have the difficult conversation. This approach works best when the situation is actually within your control. Emotion-focused coping manages your emotional response to the stressor. If you can't change the situation, you can change how you feel about it. This includes reappraisal, relaxation techniques, seeking emotional support, and yes, sometimes healthy distraction. Neither approach is universally better. The most effective copers use both, matching their strategy to the situation. Social support is one of the most powerful buffers against stress. People with strong social networks have better immune function, lower blood pressure, and longer lifespans. It's not just about having people around you. It's about feeling genuinely connected and supported. Health-promoting behaviors matter too. Exercise is essentially a wonder drug for stress. Regular physical activity reduces cortisol, boosts endorphins, improves sleep, and enhances mood. Adequate sleep, which we covered in Week 2, is critical for stress recovery. Good nutrition supports brain function and immune health. The biopsychosocial model ties it all together: your health is the product of biological processes, psychological factors like coping and personality, and social factors like relationships and socioeconomic conditions. Ignoring any one of these dimensions gives you an incomplete picture.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide7.mp3|Now we get into one of the most important and most misunderstood areas of psychology: psychological disorders. Let's start with a basic question. What makes something a disorder versus just being different? Psychologists use the four D's as a guideline. Deviance: does the behavior violate cultural norms? Distress: is the person suffering? Dysfunction: does it interfere with daily life? Danger: is there risk of harm to self or others? No single D is sufficient. A behavior has to meet several criteria to qualify as a disorder. This framework isn't perfect. Cultural norms vary. What's considered deviant in one society may be normal in another. That's why clinical judgment matters. The medical model views psychological disorders like physical diseases, with biological causes that can be diagnosed and treated. The biopsychosocial model is broader, recognizing that biology, psychology, and social factors all contribute. The DSM-5 is the diagnostic manual used by mental health professionals. It categorizes disorders, lists diagnostic criteria, and provides a common language for clinicians and researchers. But it's a tool, not a bible. It's been revised many times, and what counts as a disorder changes as our understanding evolves. And then there's the problem of labeling. Once someone receives a diagnosis, it can change how others see them and how they see themselves. David Rosenhan's classic experiment showed that once people were labeled as having schizophrenia, everything they did was interpreted through that lens. Labels are necessary for treatment, but they carry weight.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide8.mp3|Anxiety disorders are the most common category of psychological disorders. And I want to be clear: having anxiety is not the same as having an anxiety disorder. Everyone feels anxious sometimes. That's normal and adaptive. It becomes a disorder when it's persistent, excessive, and interferes with your ability to function. Generalized anxiety disorder involves chronic, free-floating worry about everything, health, money, relationships, the future, that's difficult to control and accompanied by physical symptoms like muscle tension, fatigue, and difficulty concentrating. Specific phobias are intense, irrational fears of particular objects or situations, spiders, heights, flying, blood. Social anxiety disorder involves debilitating fear of social situations where you might be judged or embarrassed. Panic disorder involves sudden, intense episodes of terror with physical symptoms so severe that people often think they're having a heart attack. Agoraphobia is the fear of situations where escape might be difficult, often developing after panic attacks. OCD involves unwanted, intrusive thoughts, the obsessions, and repetitive behaviors performed to reduce the anxiety those thoughts cause, the compulsions. PTSD develops after exposure to a traumatic event and involves flashbacks, avoidance, hyperarousal, and negative changes in mood and thinking. These disorders have biological components, including genetics and neurotransmitter imbalances, psychological factors like learned fear responses and cognitive distortions, and social factors like trauma exposure and lack of support. Treatment works, and it works well for most people, but too many people suffer in silence because of stigma.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide9.mp3|Major depressive disorder is not sadness. Let me say that again. Depression is not just being sad. It's a pervasive, persistent state that affects your mood, your thinking, your body, and your ability to function. Symptoms include depressed mood most of the day, loss of interest in things you used to enjoy, changes in sleep and appetite, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness, difficulty concentrating, and in severe cases, thoughts of death or suicide. It's the leading cause of disability worldwide, and it affects people across all demographics. Bipolar disorders involve cycling between depressive episodes and periods of mania or hypomania. Mania involves elevated mood, increased energy, decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, and often impulsive behavior. The depressive phases can be just as severe as in unipolar depression. The biological factors include genetics, neurotransmitter imbalances particularly serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine, and structural brain differences. But genes don't determine destiny. Environmental triggers, stressful life events, early adversity, and social isolation all play roles. Schizophrenia is perhaps the most misunderstood of all mental disorders. It's not split personality. It involves positive symptoms like hallucinations and delusions, things that are present that shouldn't be. Negative symptoms include flat affect, social withdrawal, and reduced motivation, things that are absent that should be present. And cognitive symptoms include impaired attention, working memory, and executive function. The dopamine hypothesis suggests that excessive dopamine activity contributes to positive symptoms, and most antipsychotic medications work by blocking dopamine receptors. But the story is more complex than that, involving glutamate, brain structure, genetics, and prenatal factors.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide10.mp3|If you or someone you care about ever needs mental health treatment, you should know what's available and what works. Psychodynamic therapy descends from Freud's approach. It focuses on gaining insight into unconscious processes and how past experiences shape current behavior. Techniques include free association, dream analysis, and working through transference, the tendency to project feelings about important people in your life onto the therapist. Modern psychodynamic therapy is more focused and time-limited than Freud's original approach. Humanistic therapy, particularly Carl Rogers' client-centered approach, creates a supportive environment where the therapist provides empathy, genuineness, and unconditional positive regard. The belief is that in such an environment, people naturally move toward growth and self-understanding. Cognitive-behavioral therapy, or CBT, is the most extensively researched form of psychotherapy. It works on the principle that thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected. If you change dysfunctional thought patterns, the cognitive part, and change maladaptive behaviors, the behavioral part, the feelings follow. Techniques include cognitive restructuring, challenging irrational thoughts, exposure therapy for anxiety disorders, and behavioral activation for depression. Group and family therapy recognize that psychological problems often exist within a social context. Here's something important from the research: across all types of therapy, the strongest predictor of good outcomes is the therapeutic alliance, the quality of the relationship between therapist and client. Empathy, trust, and a shared sense of hope matter more than the specific technique being used.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide11.mp3|Beyond talk therapy, there's the world of biomedical treatments, and this is where the biopsychology from Week 1 really comes full circle. Psychopharmacology is the use of medications to treat psychological disorders. Antidepressants, particularly SSRIs and SNRIs, work by increasing the availability of serotonin and norepinephrine in the synapse. They're effective for depression, anxiety disorders, and OCD, but they take several weeks to reach full effect, and finding the right medication and dosage often involves trial and error. Anxiolytics like benzodiazepines enhance GABA activity, producing rapid relief from anxiety, but they carry risks of dependence. Antipsychotics block dopamine receptors and are the primary treatment for schizophrenia. Newer atypical antipsychotics affect both dopamine and serotonin. Mood stabilizers, particularly lithium, are the mainstay of bipolar disorder treatment. Electroconvulsive therapy gets a bad reputation from movies, but modern ECT is administered under anesthesia, uses brief pulses, and is one of the most effective treatments for severe, treatment-resistant depression. The patient doesn't feel pain and typically has only mild, temporary memory effects. Emerging approaches include transcranial magnetic stimulation, which uses magnetic pulses to stimulate specific brain regions, deep brain stimulation for severe cases, and psychedelic-assisted therapy using substances like psilocybin, which has shown remarkable promise in early clinical trials for depression and PTSD. For many disorders, the combination of psychotherapy and medication produces better outcomes than either alone. The key is matching the treatment to the individual.
pptx/slide-audio/w5-slide12.mp3|Evidence-based practice is the principle that treatment decisions should integrate the best available research evidence, the clinician's expertise, and the patient's values and preferences. It sounds obvious, but for much of psychology's history, treatments were chosen based on the therapist's training and theoretical orientation rather than on what the evidence showed actually works. The good news is that the evidence says psychotherapy works. Meta-analyses, studies that combine the results of many individual studies, consistently show that people who receive therapy do better than about eighty percent of those who don't. CBT has the largest evidence base, but psychodynamic, humanistic, and other approaches have support for specific conditions. Cultural competence is increasingly recognized as essential. Effective treatment must account for the client's cultural background, values, beliefs, and life circumstances. A treatment approach developed and tested primarily with white, middle-class Americans may need significant adaptation for clients from different backgrounds. From neurons to social influence, from sleep to schizophrenia, human behavior is always the product of biological processes, psychological experiences, and social context.
